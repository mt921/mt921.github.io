Evolution Simulation

  • Concept
  • This is a snippet of some of my work from summer 2023 during Imperial College's Undergraduate Research Opportunities Programme [UROP].
  • In this 8 week project I constructed a web-based simulation model to test how creatures or 'agents' would behave under different conditions. The overall aim of this project was to initialise a set of randomly generated agents that would eventually converge to distinct behaviours in order to navigatedifferent environments and maximise rewards.
  • Defining Agent Structure
  • In order to create the simulation two key data structures are created in the form of a matrix and column vector:
    • \[ \mathbf{G} = \begin{bmatrix} g_{00} & g_{01} & \cdots & g_{0n} \\ g_{10} & g_{11} & \cdots & g_{1n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ g_{m0} & g_{m1} & \cdots & g_{mn} \\ \end{bmatrix}, \quad \quad \quad \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \\ \vdots \\ b_m \\ \end{bmatrix} \]
  • G represents the inherent genetic information (DNA) of each agent, it does not change in between each time step but will be important for evolution. B represents the dynamic brain state of the agent, typically changing in between each time step.
  • Time Steps
  • To perform a time-step Matrix G is multiplied by B. The output is passed through an activation function to introduce non-linearity and constrain the values within a specific range. The brain structure is updated.
    • \( \mathbf{B}_{t+1} = f(\mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{B}_t) \)
    \[ \begin{bmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \\ \vdots \\ b_m \\ \end{bmatrix}_{t+1} = f \left( \begin{bmatrix} g_{00} & g_{01} & \cdots & g_{0n} \\ g_{10} & g_{11} & \cdots & g_{1n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ g_{m0} & g_{m1} & \cdots & g_{mn} \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \\ \vdots \\ b_m \\ \end{bmatrix}_{t} \right) \]
  • Several elements of B are assigned as output neurons and used to extract signals i.e. will cause the agent to move forward, rotate etc. Similarly several neurons can be assigned as inputs, allowing the agent to take in information about the environment.
  • Programming Behaviours
  • In order to prove useful behaviours were possible, several case studies of useful behaviour were defined. In these simple simulations agents can only rotate or speed up and the only information they can access is either their position, speed, direction or distance from other agents or simulation boundaries.

  • Training Behaviours
  • The agents in the previous simulations all have the same pre-defined structures, however the next challenge is to be able to randomly generate agents and train them to achieve these specfic goals.
  • In order to tackle this challenge a tournament simulation architecture is defined where agents are randomly generated, the system is allowed to progress for a set number of timesteps and then agents are evaluated.
  • The DNA structures of most successful agents can be extracted, mutated and used to create new agents in the next tournament run. This process draws on the ideas of genetic algorithms and can be used to eventually train agents to aquire the behaviours shown above.
  • Continuous Simulation
  • The goal of this project is to initialise a set of randomly generated agents that eventually achieve useful behaviours without being manually trained. In order to achieve this a continuous simulation architecture was created where agents are free to move about, consume smaller agents in the system, clone themselves or grow.
