Evolution Simulation

  • Concept
  • This is a snippet of some of my work from summer 2023 during Imperial College's Undergraduate Research Opportunities Programme [UROP].
  • In this 8 week project I constructed a web-based simulation model to test how creatures or 'agents' would behave under different conditions. The overall aim of this project was to initialise a set of randomly generated agents that would eventually converge to distinct behaviours in order to navigate different environments and maximise rewards.
  • Defining Agent Structure
  • In order to create the simulation two key structures are defined:
    • \[ \mathbf{G} = \begin{bmatrix} g_{00} & g_{01} & \cdots & g_{0n} \\ g_{10} & g_{11} & \cdots & g_{1n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ g_{m0} & g_{m1} & \cdots & g_{mn} \\ \end{bmatrix}, \quad \quad \quad \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \\ \vdots \\ b_m \\ \end{bmatrix} \]
  • G represents the genetic information (DNA) of each agent, it does not change in between each time step but will be important for evolution. B represents the dynamic brain state of the agent, typically changing in between each time step.
  • Time Steps
  • To perform a time step Matrix G is multiplied by B. The output is passed through an activation function to introduce non-linearity and constrain the values within a specific range. This allows the agent's brain structure to be updated.
    • \( \mathbf{B}_{t+1} = f(\mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{B}_t) \)
    \[ \begin{bmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \\ \vdots \\ b_m \\ \end{bmatrix}_{t+1} = f \left( \begin{bmatrix} g_{00} & g_{01} & \cdots & g_{0n} \\ g_{10} & g_{11} & \cdots & g_{1n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ g_{m0} & g_{m1} & \cdots & g_{mn} \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \\ \vdots \\ b_m \\ \end{bmatrix}_{t} \right) \]
  • Each elements of B is a neuron which will have a level of actiavtion. Neurons can be assigned as inputs or outputs, where outputs will trigger agents to perform an action (e.g. move, rotate etc.) and inputs will allow the agent to take in information about the environment.
  • Programming Behaviours
  • In order to prove useful behaviours were possible, several pre-programmed case studies were created. In these simple simulations agents can only rotate or speed up and the only information they can access is either their position, speed, direction or distance from other agents or simulation boundaries.

  • Training Behaviours
  • The agents in the previous simulations all have the same pre-defined structures. The next challenge is to randomly generate agents and train them to achieve these specfic goals.
  • The simulation is adpated to follow a tournament architecture where agents are randomly generated, allowed to progress for a number of time steps and then evaluated.
  • The DNA structures of the most successful agents can be extracted, mutated and used to create new agents in the next tournament run. This process draws on the ideas of genetic algorithms and can be used to eventually train agents to aquire the behaviours shown above.
  • Continuous Simulation
  • The overarching goal of this project is to initialise a set of randomly generated agents that eventually achieve useful or interesting behaviours without manual input. In order to achieve this a continuous simulation architecture was created where agents are free to move about, consume smaller 'prey' agents in the system, clone themselves or grow. This allows for multiple generations to progress without user input.
